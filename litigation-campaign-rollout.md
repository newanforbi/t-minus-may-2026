Executive Summary and Tactical Overview
The coordinated initiation of six distinct legal actions across federal and state jurisdictions in
May 2026 represents a highly sophisticated, multi-front litigation campaign engineered to
dismantle arbitrary administrative control. This strategy is designed to apply overwhelming
administrative, legal, and operational pressure on the California Department of Corrections and
Rehabilitation (CDCR) and the California Department of Motor Vehicles (DMV). By
synchronizing filings across the United States District Court for the Eastern District of California
(EDCA), the Sacramento County Superior Court, and the Santa Clara County Superior Court,
the campaign forces the State of California to defend its policies and personnel on multiple
geographic and doctrinal fronts simultaneously.
This strategy abandons the traditional, serialized approach to pro se litigation, which often
languishes in procedural backlogs. Instead, it utilizes a highly calibrated methodology,
leveraging the immediate payment of federal civil filing fees to bypass the lengthy and restrictive
screening processes traditionally applied to In Forma Pauperis (IFP) applications under 28
U.S.C. § 1915(e)(2). This procedural pivot ensures the immediate issuance of summonses,
placing the power of compulsory process directly into the plaintiff's hands. This allows for
simultaneous strikes against CDCR Headquarters, DMV Headquarters, and the Stockton Parole
Office on predetermined dates.
The following analysis provides an exhaustive, granular breakdown of the campaign's legal
architecture. It details the procedural mechanics of transitioning from IFP to paid status, the
geospatial and statutory logistics for service of process, and the profound second- and
third-order systemic impacts anticipated from this strategically disorienting rollout. The
overarching objective is to expose and rectify the systemic weaponization of administrative
authority, ultimately securing the plaintiff's release from unlawful parole supervision.

Case One Analysis: Newanforbi v. Dodd, et al.
The first pillar of the May 8 rollout is a state tort action venued in the Sacramento County
Superior Court, which attacks the procedural and substantive deficiencies of a November 2025
discharge review process. The named defendants include Parole Administrator Keely Dodd,
Unit Supervisor Gary Noguchi, Parole Agent Joseelyn Rojo, an unidentified Doe 1, and the
CDCR itself. The causes of action are rooted in the California Government Claims Act,
specifically alleging Breach of Mandatory Duty under Government Code §§ 815.2 and 815.6,
alongside common law claims for False Imprisonment, Intentional Infliction of Emotional
Distress, and Negligence.
The factual predicate of this case centers on the deliberate subversion of Penal Code §
3001(a)(2), which mandates discharge from parole after one year of continuous supervision
absent a lawful, individualized good-cause finding. The complaint alleges that the defendants
fabricated categorical eligibility rules to deny this discharge, specifically inventing a "10-year
parole term" exclusion and a "6.5-year" minimum supervision requirement that exist nowhere in
enacted law or regulation. This represents the enforcement of an illegal underground regulation.
Furthermore, the action highlights severe administrative recklessness. The CDCR Form
1502-DR used to deny the discharge was an obsolete revision (Rev. 01/22) rather than the
mandated November 2024 revision, bypassing newly enacted containment team protocols.
Alarmingly, the report ratified by Supervisor Noguchi and Administrator Dodd contained a
copy-paste transcription error referencing an entirely different parolee named "Eagan,
" providing
documentary proof that no individualized review occurred.
The strategic utility of filing this action in state court is paramount. By relying on the California
Government Claims Act, the plaintiff bypasses the stringent qualified immunity defenses that
frequently stall federal civil rights litigation. Naming the CDCR as a defendant for vicarious
liability ensures the state is financially anchored to the tort claims, which seek substantial
compensatory damages. The plaintiff has documented economic losses exceeding $109,408 in
lost Commercial Driver's License (CDL) wages between 2022 and 2024, measured against a
but-for earning capacity of $72,000 per year, with ongoing losses accruing at approximately
$6,000 per month. Furthermore, the complaint asserts that Defendant Rojo failed to serve the
finalized report, depriving the plaintiff of timely administrative appellate rights, thereby solidifying
the false imprisonment timeline.
Case Two Analysis: Newanforbi v. Macomber
Simultaneous to the state tort filing, a federal civil rights action under 42 U.S.C. § 1983 is
launched in the Eastern District of California against Jeff Macomber, the Secretary of the CDCR.
Sued exclusively in his official capacity, this action serves as a direct strike against the highest
level of the department's administrative hierarchy. The core legal theory rests on the Fourteenth
Amendment's Procedural Due Process Clause, challenging the systemic enforcement of
unconstitutional policies.
The factual foundation of this suit is the ongoing enforcement of CDCR Policy 19-03
(Amended). The complaint argues that this internal policy imposes rigid, categorical temporal
minimums on parole retention, explicitly prohibiting discharge consideration before 6.5 years of
active supervision for those with 10-year terms. This rigid framework directly conflicts with the
individualized clinical discretion mandated by Penal Code § 3008(d) and the newly adopted Title
15, California Code of Regulations § 3574, which went into effect in April 2025. The new
regulations explicitly permit early completion of sex offender management programs and
establish objective thresholds for retention, such as a Level of Service/Case Management
Inventory (LS/CMI) score of 11 or higher. Despite the plaintiff possessing an LS/CMI score of 5,
placing him at a "Below Average Risk,
" agents relied exclusively on Policy 19-03's mathematical
formula to deny discharge, abandoning the required individualized assessment.
The strategic brilliance of this federal pleading lies in its requested relief. By seeking solely
prospective declaratory and injunctive relief under the Ex parte Young doctrine, the suit cleanly
bypasses Eleventh Amendment sovereign immunity. It forces Secretary Macomber to publicly
defend a statewide policy that explicitly contradicts his department's own newly promulgated
regulations. The failure of the CDCR headquarters to disseminate directives retracting Policy
19-03 following the April 2025 regulatory amendments demonstrates a systemic supervisory
failure, making Macomber the necessary and proper target for equitable relief.
Case Three Analysis: Newanforbi v. Urrea, et al.
The third component of the May 8 rollout is another federal action in the EDCA, addressing a
severe abuse of power: the weaponization of parole conditions to manufacture arbitrary
detention. This suit names Agent Urrea, Unit Supervisor Gary Noguchi, and Secretary
Macomber. It asserts 42 U.S.C. § 1983 claims under the Fourth Amendment for unreasonable
seizure and over-detention, alongside Fourteenth Amendment claims for violations of
procedural and substantive due process.
The narrative centers on an egregious incident occurring between November 1 and November
6, 2024. Following a DUI arrest by the California Highway Patrol, the plaintiff was booked into
Santa Rita Jail. On the morning of November 1, jail personnel informed the plaintiff that the
District Attorney had not filed charges and he was otherwise eligible for immediate release,
causing his right to liberty to vest. At that precise moment, Agent Urrea placed a parole hold,
overriding the release and initiating a new seizure under the Fourth Amendment. The pretext for
this hold was an alleged violation of Special Condition No. 063—a "Halloween curfew"
mandating the plaintiff remain indoors with exterior lights off and refrain from holiday activities.
The constitutional fatal flaw in this detention is the complete absence of a rational nexus. Under
California law, specifically People v. Lent, parole conditions must be reasonably related to the
subject's commitment offense or future criminality. The plaintiff's underlying commitment offense
involved an adult victim, bearing absolutely no connection to minors, trick-or-treating, or
holiday-specific scenarios. CDCR's own documentation assigned this condition "Reason Code
08,
" explicitly indicating it is for offenders with "similar crimes" or "similar criminal behavior"
.
Because the condition was facially invalid as applied to the plaintiff, any hold predicated upon it
lacked probable cause.
This pretextual hold resulted in five days of arbitrary "black box" incarceration. The plaintiff was
subjected to menacing conditions, denied any prompt probable cause hearing within 48 hours
(violating the Valdivia injunction framework), and received no written notice of the evidence
against him (violating Morrissey v. Brewer parameters). Furthermore, this unconstitutional
detention caused the plaintiff to miss the first four shifts of his new employment as a commercial
truck driver with Centerline Drivers, resulting in direct wage losses between $1,534 and $2,301,
and placing his probationary employment in severe jeopardy.
Strategically, this suit attacks the CDCR's annual "Operation Boo" enforcement sweep,
transforming an individual grievance into a systemic indictment. By naming Noguchi for his
ministerial act of approving the facially invalid condition without a nexus review, and Urrea for
executing the unconstitutional seizure, the plaintiff establishes localized liability. Naming
Macomber elevates the claim, attacking the systemic failure of the CDCR to require
nexus-certification forms or prompt neutral reviews before allowing administrative holds to
override judicial release mechanisms.

Procedural Mechanics: The Tactical Pivot from IFP to
Paid Status
A cornerstone of this campaign's strategic architecture is the deliberate circumvention of the
federal In Forma Pauperis (IFP) screening process. The plaintiff's federal pleadings (Macomber,
Urrea, Candelaria, and Rojo) were initially submitted pending IFP approval. Under 28 U.S.C. §
1915(e)(2), when a pro se plaintiff proceeds IFP, the district court is statutorily mandated to
conduct a preliminary screening to ensure the complaint is not frivolous, malicious, or failing to
state a claim upon which relief can be granted. In the heavily backlogged Eastern District of
California, this screening process operates as a significant bottleneck. It can delay the issuance
of a summons by weeks or months, effectively paralyzing a litigation campaign's momentum
and depriving the plaintiff of the element of surprise.
To execute the strategically disorienting rollout planned for May 2026, the plaintiff must reclaim
control of the procedural timeline. This is achieved by abandoning the IFP requests and paying
the $405 civil filing fee for each federal action on their designated target dates. Once the full
filing fee is tendered, the statutory screening requirement of § 1915(e)(2) is bypassed. The
Clerk of Court is then obligated under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 4(b) to issue the
summons (Form AO-440) immediately, placing the power to initiate service of process directly
into the hands of the plaintiff.

For the cases venued in the Sacramento Division of the EDCA, the procedural steps for

issue the official, court-sealed summons, officially authorizing the commencement of the
service of process phase.
Sacramento Superior Court Fee Waiver Mechanics
Conversely, the state tort action (Newanforbi v. Dodd) relies on an approved fee waiver in the
Sacramento County Superior Court. The mechanics of state court fee waivers differ significantly
from the federal system and offer distinct procedural advantages for this campaign.
1. Documentation and E-Filing: The plaintiff utilizes Judicial Council forms FW-001
(Request to Waive Court Fees) and FW-003 (Order on Court Fee Waiver). The
Sacramento Superior Court's electronic filing system permits the simultaneous
submission of the case-initiating document (the Complaint) and the FW-001 fee waiver
application within the exact same digital transaction. Pro se litigants are permitted to
utilize certified Electronic Filing Service Providers (EFSPs) to transmit these documents
24 hours a day.
2. Filing Deadlines and Approval Insulation: Documents submitted electronically between
12:00 a.m. and 11:59 p.m. on a court day are deemed filed that same day. This parameter
allows precise temporal control over the May 8, 2026, filing target. Because the fee waiver
is already noted as approved for this action, the state tort avoids the hazardous 10-day
statutory window during which a denied waiver must be cured by payment to prevent the
voiding of all filed documents. The action is instantly perfected, and the summons can be
issued for immediate service.
Geospatial Logistics and Service of Process Matrix
The physical execution of the litigation campaign relies on the precise, simultaneous service of
process across three highly secure, geographically distinct administrative locations. The
element of surprise is maximized when high-ranking officials in Sacramento and field-level
agents in Stockton are served with sweeping federal and state lawsuits on the exact same day.
Service of process upon state employees and public entities in California is governed by both
the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure (Rule 4) and the California Code of Civil Procedure (CCP).
Under CCP § 416.50, a public entity may be served by delivering the summons and complaint
to the clerk, secretary, president, or other head of its governing body. When personal delivery to
an individual defendant cannot be effectuated with reasonable diligence, CCP § 415.20 permits
substituted service. This is accomplished by leaving a copy of the documents during usual office
hours at the defendant's usual place of business with a person who is apparently in charge,
informing them of the contents, and subsequently mailing a copy via first-class mail to that same
address. Service is deemed complete on the 10th day after mailing.

7. Service deadlines, process servers, and cost-saving
strategies
Federal service deadline: 90 days
Under FRCP 4(m), defendants must be served within 90 days after filing the complaint. If
service is not completed, the court must dismiss without prejudice or order service within a
specified time. Legal Information Institute Good cause extends the deadline as of right; even
without good cause, courts have discretion to grant extensions. LegalClarity For IFP cases,
the 90 days effectively runs from the date of summons issuance (not complaint filing), since
the plaintiff cannot serve until the court screens and issues summons.
California state service deadline: 60 days practical, 3 years mandatory
Two overlapping deadlines apply. CCP § 583.210 requires service within three years of
filing — this is mandatory with no judicial discretion to extend except by written stipulation
or statutory exclusion. California Rules of Court, Rule 3.110(b) requires service and filing
of proofs of service within 60 days of filing. This is an administrative rule; non-compliance
triggers an OSC re: dismissal at the Case Management Conference, but extensions are
available for good cause.
Who can serve process
Under CCP § 414.10, any person who is at least 18 years old and not a party to the action
may serve process in California. Justia No registration is required. Under FRCP 4(c)(2),
the same rule applies in federal court. However, a registered process server (Business &
Professions Code § 22350) provides a significant advantage: under California Evidence
Code § 647, a registered server’s proof of service creates a presumption of valid service
— courts are far more likely to accept it without challenge.
Waiver of service: the cost-free federal option
FRCP 4(d) offers a powerful cost-saving mechanism. Crushendo The plaintiff mails each
defendant: (1) Form AO 398 (Notice of Lawsuit and Request to Waive Service), (2) a copy

prepaid return envelope. The defendant has 30 days to return the signed waiver. If they
waive, they get 60 days to answer (instead of 21). Crushendo If they refuse without good
cause, the court must impose the costs of formal service on the defendant. Crushendo
Critical limitation: waiver of service is available for individual-capacity defendants but
not for state officers in official capacity — those must be formally served under FRCP 4(i)
and state law (AG service). Department of Justice For the Newanforbi cases, waiver of
